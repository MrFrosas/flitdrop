Why it fails

Why Quick Share Struggles With iPhone

Quick Share, formerly Nearby Share, is genuinely good inside the Android and Windows world. The trouble starts the moment an iPhone is involved.

  • 1

    Not built for iPhone

    Quick Share was designed for Android, Samsung and Windows. The iPhone was never a first class target, so any route to it is partial at best.

  • 2

    Bluetooth visibility windows

    Discovery leans on Bluetooth being visible at the right moment, so the two phones often simply do not see each other before the window closes.

  • 3

    Interop or QR fallback

    Reaching an iPhone can depend on an AirDrop style interop on some phones or a QR fallback, which is inconsistent and depends on which devices you own.

  • 4

    A Wi-Fi answer instead

    Flitdrop skips Bluetooth and vendor interop entirely: both phones transfer over your local Wi-Fi, so the connection does not depend on a fragile window.

Private by design

Your Files Stay on Your Wi-Fi

Flitdrop is not an internet or cloud service. Transfers go directly device to device over your local Wi-Fi, everything is end to end encrypted, and there is no account, so nothing is ever uploaded to a server.

It is source available, so anyone can inspect how the sharing works, and it stays free for local transfers on your own network.

Frequently asked

Why is Quick Share to iPhone not working?

Quick Share, formerly Nearby Share, was built for Android, Samsung and Windows, not for the iPhone. Its reach to an iPhone is limited and depends on short Bluetooth visibility windows and, in some cases, an AirDrop style interop or a QR fallback, so it is easy for the two phones not to find each other. Flitdrop avoids all of that: it sends Android to iPhone over your local Wi-Fi with no dependence on Bluetooth or same vendor interop.

Can you use Quick Share with an iPhone?

Only in a limited way. Quick Share is designed for Android, Samsung and Windows devices, and the iPhone was never a first class target, so any path to an iPhone tends to be partial and depends on Bluetooth being discoverable at the right moment. For a reliable Android to iPhone transfer, Flitdrop is the dependable route because it uses your local Wi-Fi rather than Bluetooth pairing.

How do I send files from Android to iPhone reliably?

Install the Flitdrop app on any computer, open Flitdrop on both phones in the browser after a one time QR scan, keep both phones on the same Wi-Fi, and send. There is no Bluetooth pairing to fail and no vendor interop to depend on, so full quality photos, files and folders move both ways between Android and iPhone.

Do I need an app on either phone?

No. Flitdrop installs only on the computer, on Windows, Mac or Linux, and it auto updates. Both phones, Android and iPhone, install nothing: each one scans a QR code once and then uses its browser as a lightweight web app, so there is nothing to install on either phone.

Is it free?

Yes. Flitdrop is free for local transfers on your own network, with no subscription and no ads. You can download it from the official GitHub releases page.

Is it private?

Yes. Flitdrop is not an internet or cloud service. Transfers go directly device to device over your local Wi-Fi, everything is end to end encrypted, and there is no account, so your files never pass through a server.
