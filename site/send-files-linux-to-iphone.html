Send Files From Linux to iPhone (No App on the Phone, Free)

Linux never got AirDrop, and Warpinator, the usual Linux tool, has no real iPhone support. Flitdrop gives Linux a native app and the iPhone needs nothing beyond a browser and a one time QR scan. Send files, photos and folders over your local Wi-Fi, end to end encrypted, at full quality.

Download Flitdrop See how it works

To send files from Linux to an iPhone, install the Flitdrop app on your Linux computer, scan its QR code once with the iPhone camera, and send. The iPhone uses its browser, so nothing is installed on the phone. Files, photos and whole folders move directly over your local Wi-Fi, end to end encrypted, at full quality, with no account and no cloud round trip. It works because Linux has no AirDrop, and the common Linux tools like Warpinator and KDE Connect are built for Linux and Android, not iOS.

The gap

Why Linux and iPhone Are So Hard to Bridge

AirDrop is Apple only, and the good Linux tools aim at Android, so getting a file from Linux onto an iPhone usually means a clunky detour. Here is the honest picture.

  • 1

    No AirDrop on Linux

    AirDrop only works between Apple devices and never came to Linux, so there is no built in one gesture way to send a file from a Linux desktop to an iPhone.

  • 2

    Warpinator skips iOS

    Warpinator is great for Linux to Linux and Linux to Android and has an official Android app, but there is no official iPhone or iPad app, so it does not reach iOS.

  • 3

    KDE Connect is Android first

    KDE Connect pairs Linux with Android very well, but its iOS support is limited by what Apple allows, so it is not a reliable Linux to iPhone file path.

  • 4

    Cloud detours are clunky

    Emailing photos to yourself or bouncing files through a cloud service is slow, needs the internet, and often shrinks or recompresses your photos along the way.

Getting started

How to Send From Linux to iPhone

Install the app on your Linux computer once, pair your iPhone with a single scan, then send over your Wi-Fi.

  1. 1

    Install on Linux

    Install the lightweight Flitdrop app once on your Linux computer; it auto updates and there is nothing to install on the iPhone.

  2. 2

    Scan once to pair

    Scan the QR code one time with the iPhone camera to pair; the iPhone simply uses its browser as a lightweight web app, with nothing from the App Store.

  3. 3

    Send over your Wi-Fi

    Both devices stay on the same local Wi-Fi and transfer peer to peer, so files and folders move fast, at full quality, and stay off the cloud.

Everything is end to end encrypted, with no account to create and privacy first by design.

When to use each

Warpinator, KDE Connect and Flitdrop

Warpinator and KDE Connect are genuinely good tools; they just were not built for the iPhone. Here is when each one is the right choice.

  • 1

    Use Warpinator for Linux and Android

    Warpinator is open source, local and free, and it shines for Linux to Linux and Linux to Android transfers with its official Android app. If no iPhone is involved, it is a fine pick.

  • 2

    Use KDE Connect for Linux and Android

    KDE Connect does far more than files between Linux and Android: notifications, clipboard, remote control. For a Linux plus Android household it is excellent, but its iOS side is limited.

  • 3

    Use Flitdrop for Linux and iPhone

    When an iPhone is on one end, Flitdrop is the natural pick: a native Linux app, the iPhone browser with nothing to install, local and end to end encrypted, folders and clipboard too.

  • 4

    One tool for every device

    Flitdrop also covers iPhone, Android and Samsung on the phone side and Windows, Mac and Linux on the computer side, so it doubles as the Android bridge if you want a single tool.

Private by design

Your Files Stay on Your Wi-Fi

Flitdrop is not an internet or cloud service. Transfers go directly from your Linux computer to the iPhone over your local Wi-Fi, everything is end to end encrypted, and there is no account, so nothing is ever uploaded to a server or bounced through iCloud.

Flitdrop is source available, so anyone can inspect how the sharing works, and it stays free for local transfers on your own network.

Frequently asked

How do I send files from Linux to an iPhone?

Install the Flitdrop app on your Linux computer, open it, and scan the QR code once with your iPhone camera to pair. The iPhone opens a lightweight web app in its browser, and then you send files, photos and whole folders from Linux to the iPhone over your local Wi-Fi, end to end encrypted and at full quality.

Does Warpinator work with iOS?

Not really. Warpinator is excellent for Linux to Linux and Linux to Android, and there is an official Android app, but there is no official Warpinator app for iPhone or iPad, so it is not a dependable way to reach an iOS device. Flitdrop fills that gap because the iPhone needs nothing installed beyond scanning a QR code in its browser.

Is there an AirDrop for Linux and iPhone?

AirDrop is Apple only and never came to Linux, and the usual Linux tools like Warpinator and KDE Connect are aimed at Linux and Android rather than iOS. Flitdrop is the closest thing to an AirDrop that bridges Linux and iPhone: a native Linux app on one side, the iPhone browser on the other, everything local and end to end encrypted.

Do I install anything on the iPhone?

No. Only the Linux computer installs the Flitdrop app, which auto updates. The iPhone installs nothing from the App Store: it just uses its browser as a lightweight web app after a one time QR scan, so there is nothing to sign in to and nothing to keep updated on the phone.

Is it free and private?

Yes. Flitdrop is free for local transfers on your own network, with no account and no subscription. Files move directly between your Linux computer and the iPhone over your local Wi-Fi, everything is end to end encrypted, and nothing is uploaded to a server or the cloud, so there is no round trip through iCloud or email.

Does it keep full quality?

Yes. Flitdrop sends photos, videos and files at full quality, without the recompression or shrinking that happens when you email photos to yourself or bounce them through a cloud service. What lands on the iPhone or on Linux is the original file, byte for byte.
